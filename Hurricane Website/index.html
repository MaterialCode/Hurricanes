Hurricanes

Introduction

A hurricane is a tropical storm characterised by winds that have sustained a speed of 120 kilometres per hour or higher. The eye of the storm is typically 30-50 kilometres wide and may extend over 650km. Hurricanes pose a number of hazards, including heavy rainfall, strong winds and storm surges. Hurricanes are common in the ocean and don't cause much destruction. However, when they start moving towards land, they can be extremely dangerous and cause significant damage. The hurricane's spiralling winds are incredibly powerful, with speeds reaching up to 320kmph. These winds can uproot entire trees and demolish buildings. Hurricanes revolve around a circular centre known as the "eye," where it is generally peaceful with no clouds. Surrounding the eye is the eye wall - the most perilous part of the hurricane, with the strongest winds, thickest clouds, and heaviest rain!

F.A.Q

Frequently Asked Questions

  • How do hurricanes form?

    Hurricanes are powerful storms that form over warm ocean waters near the equator. They begin as tropical waves, which gather warm, moist air that rises into the atmosphere. As the air cools and condenses, it forms clouds, releasing heat that fuels the storm. Winds spiral inward towards the center, creating a hurricane's eye, where the strongest winds are found. Hurricanes can travel thousands of miles and last for several weeks, causing widespread damage and loss of life.

  • Why do hurricanes form?

    They form for many reasons but here is a list of the most common.

    1. Warm ocean waters: Hurricanes are fueled by the warm, moist air over tropical oceans, typically with surface temperatures above 80 degrees Fahrenheit. This warm air provides the necessary energy to drive the storm's upward motion and subsequent condensation.
    2. Uplift: The upward motion of air is crucial for hurricane formation. This upward motion can be initiated by several factors, such as converging air currents along fronts or the presence of landmasses that deflect winds upward.
    3. Low atmospheric pressure: Hurricanes are characterized by a central area of low atmospheric pressure, often referred to as the "eye." This low pressure creates an upward suction that draws in warm, moist air from surrounding areas.
    4. Wind shear:Wind shear, the change in wind direction or speed with height, plays a crucial role in hurricane development. Moderate wind shear tends to strengthen hurricanes, while strong wind shear can weaken or dissipate them.
    5. Environmental factors: Other factors that contribute to hurricane formation include large-scale atmospheric patterns, such as the presence of upper-level troughs and the strength of the trade winds.

  • How to survive a hurricane?

    Hurricane preparedness starts before the storm strikes. Gather an emergency kit, make an evacuation plan, secure your home, and stay informed about weather updates. During the storm, remain indoors, stay away from windows and doors, and avoid downed power lines. After the storm, continue to monitor weather reports, avoid flooded areas, report damage, and help your community.

  • How are hurricanes rated

    The Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale rates hurricanes on a scale of 1 to 5 based on their sustained wind speed, which provides an estimate of the potential damage to property. Hurricanes that reach Category 3 or higher are considered to be major. Each season, an average of 10.1 named storms occur, with 5.9 becoming hurricanes and 2.5 major hurricanes, Category 3 or higher on the Saffir-Simpson scale.
